by David Mara, November 1997

The Early Years (1866-1912)

Central High School was established in 1866, making it the oldest continuously operating public high school in the state of Minnesota. The first building was downtown at 7th and Jackson Street. It burned down in 1881 and got rebuilt on the same site. That second building served until 1912 when the current structure was built at Marshall Avenue and Lexington Parkway.

The 1912 building was designed by architect Clarence H. Johnston Sr., the same guy who designed a bunch of buildings at the University of Minnesota. The style is Collegiate Gothic and when it opened it had this really cool castle-looking entrance. The building had a 1400 seat auditorium, a swimming pool, and a huge basement with boiler rooms, maintenance shops, and storage. In 1979 they did a big renovation (Ellerbe Architects) and covered up the castle exterior with cement and gutted most of the interior but a LOT of the original structure is still there behind the new walls. Theres even the original cafeteria with two levels still intact behind a storage area. You can see the old floor tiles and the mezzanine where seniors used to sit and watch the underclassmen eat lunch.

The sub-basement has a shooting range that operated until 1964. The floor used to be dirt and sand but they cemented over it a few years ago. Most people know about the shooting range. What most people DON'T know about is what's past it.

Growth and Expansion (1912-1940)

Through the 1920s and 30s, Central was THE high school in St. Paul. Enrollment peaked at over 2,500 students. The WPA built the athletic stadium in the early 1940s. Underground, the building was expanded too. The coal heating system required huge storage areas and the sub-basement was dug out for additional boiler infrastructure.

Heres the thing though. According to maintenance records I found in the school archives, there were three levels below ground by 1935. Not two. Three:

  • Basement Level 1 - Storage, maintenance shop, boiler room, the old original cafeteria (still partially accessible if you know where to look)
  • Basement Level 2 - The shooting range, old coal room, B2 maintenance corridor (partially sealed off during the 79 renovation)
  • Sub-Basement Level 3 - PURPOSE NOT LISTED IN ANY OFFICIAL RECORD
The blueprints at the Minnesota Historical Society on Kellogg Blvd only show two basement levels. The city building department records only show two levels. But the janitor logs from 1935 through 1943 CLEARLY reference a third level. Head Janitor Earl Whitmore writes about going down to "the sub-level" multiple times. He describes it as a large open space with concrete walls and a floor that "sweats" even in summer. He said the air down there tasted like copper and old meat.

The War Years (1940-1945)

This is where it gets strange.

I found records at the St. Paul Public Library that mention Central High being considered as a potential civil defense site during WWII. There are references to "sub-surface preparations" in city council minutes from 1941. Nothing specific about what those preparations involved.

Then, in the school archives, I found janitor logs from Earl Whitmore. Most of them are boring - supply orders, repair notes, that kind of thing. But starting in September 1942, the entries change:

From Earl Whitmore's Maintenance Log, September 18, 1942:
"Men from the city came again today. Third time this month. They went down to the sub-level and were there for six hours. They brought equipment I did not recognize. They told me not to log their visits but I am logging them anyway because it is my building and I need to know what goes on in it."
October 3, 1942:
"The sounds have started again. The boiler men say it is just the pipes settling in the cold but the sounds come from BELOW the boiler room. I have been head janitor here for 11 years and I know what the pipes sound like. This is not the pipes."
November 22, 1942:
"Robert Halvorsen did not come to work today. He was supposed to do the evening shift in the boiler room. Mrs. Halvorsen called the school and said he never came home on Friday. I checked his locker and his coat and lunch pail were still there. I told Principal Ward and he said he would 'handle it.' He told me not to contact the police."
December 14, 1942:
"Another one. Margaret Svensson from the cleaning staff. She was assigned to sweep the lower corridors on Wednesday evening. She did not sign out. Her time card is still in the rack. That makes TWO since September. Principal Ward will not speak to me about it. He told me to stop asking questions and to stay away from the sub-level. He said it was a 'matter of public safety.' I do not understand what public safety has to do with my staff disappearing."
there were more. i found four more names in the logs through february 1943. six people total. none of them were reported missing to the police. i checked.
February 7, 1943:
"They sealed the sub-level entrance today. Poured concrete over the stairwell. The men from the city supervised. They brought a new door for the B2 corridor - steel, with a lock I do not have a key for. When I asked why, the foreman told me it was for 'structural reasons.' Structural reasons do not require armed men standing watch while you pour concrete. I am putting in my resignation on Monday."


I scanned the last page of Whitmore's log at the St. Paul Public Library. Here it is:


Scan of the final page of Earl Whitmore's maintenance log. The handwriting is shakier than his earlier entries. You can see where the pen pressed harder into the paper on certain words. There is a water stain on the right side and the paper has yellowed significantly. I scanned this at 150 dpi on the Microtek at the Rice Street library.

That is the last entry from Earl Whitmore. According to school records, he resigned on February 10, 1943. I can find no further mention of the sub-basement in any official school records after this date.

I asked Ms. Patterson about it and she said she had no idea what I was talking about. I showed her the janitor logs and she got really quiet. She closed the binder and put it back in the box and told me I should focus on "the positive aspects of Central's history" for my project. Then she asked me not to come back to the archives for a while. When I came back the next week the box with Whitmore's logs was gone. She said it had been "sent to the district office for cataloguing." I called the district office on University Avenue. They said they had no record of receiving any materials from Central High.

Somebody moved that box because of what I found in it.

Post-War to Present (1945-1997)

Central continued to operate normally through the rest of the 20th century. Enrollment declined in the 1970s and 80s but has stabilized. The building was added to the National Register of Historic Places in 1986.

The current basement is used for storage, the boiler room, and some maintenance areas. There is a locked steel door at the end of the B2 corridor that nobody seems to have a key for. The current maintenance staff says it "probably leads to an old coal room or something."

I don't think it leads to a coal room.

I'm going to find out what's behind that door.
